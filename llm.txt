A Trace-Language Theory of Agents:
Goal-Driven Type-0 Producers Bounded by Data-Driven Type-3 Consumers

Koohestani, Li, Podkopaev, and Izadi recently asked, "Are agents
just automata?" koohestani2025automata and answered in
the affirmative by establishing a memory-architecture equivalence
between agentic AI classes and the levels of the Chomsky
hierarchy.  We accept their answer and pose the more useful
question: can the Chomsky type of one agent be used to
bound the Chomsky type of another, in the manner of the
classical producer-consumer problem?  This paper answers
yes, with software verification and validation
techniques inferred into a Data-Driven Type-3 verifier
(consumer), and the idealised theoretical apparatus of automata
built into a Goal-Driven Type-0 producer.  The verification
loop must end in decidability, otherwise the classical halting
problem of Turing turing1936computable re-asserts itself
on every iteration.  Decidability is enforced here by the regular
language of the Data-Driven verifier, realised as the
deterministic finite automaton of an Aho-Corasick keyword
index aho1975corasick compiled from the unit-test
catalogue, with a final human verification and validation step
that closes the loop in the bounded case.

We give a self-contained formal theory of the result.  An agent is
identified with its trace language: the set of finite
strings of effectful operations the agent emits across the input
domain.  Within this theory we define a single Goal-Driven Type-0
agent whose trace language is recursively
enumerable robinson1949definability,davis1961decision and a
single Data-Driven Type-3 verifier whose trace language is
regular rabin1959finite,chomsky1956three,chomsky1959certain.
The Goal-Driven agent is tamed by the Type-3 verifier in the
precise sense that only those traces accepted by the verifier are
emitted to disk; from this binding, the Type-2 (pushdown), the
indexed-grammar aho1968indexed, and Type-1 (linear-bounded)
sub-agents emerge as canonical projections.  We further show that
a Type-3 agent may itself act as a producer, backward-inferring
constrained changes to the tape, provided the inference terminates
in a decidable verdict.

The single start language that initiates the system is
given in data/tracelanguageoriginal.csv: twelve
sub-agents and their seed action alphabets.  Under the binding
above, the start language recursively enumerates itself onto the
Spaceship Titanic task drawn from the public
MLE-bench benchmark, producing the empirical trace
recorded in data/tracelanguagekaggle.csv: thirteen
sub-agents (the thirteenth, kaggletrainer, is
emergent - it exists only after the binding has fired)
and a strictly longer action string per agent.  The Goal-Driven
agent's trace language is, on paper, recursively enumerable; the
physical execution is bounded by the finite tape and finite memory
of a single laptop, and the realised machine is therefore a
(very large) linear-bounded automaton.  We prove the idealisation
sound: any trace the idealised Type-0 agent can emit is
producible by the bounded machine up to the laptop's tape limit,
and the verified trace language is closed under intersection at
the verifier's Chomsky level (Type-3) so verification cannot
exceed the bounded machine's intrinsic complexity.

We instantiate the theory on the Spaceship Titanic task
and report a leaderboard placement of  out of  teams

baselines reported on github.com/openai/mle-bench.  The

Introduction

The contemporary literature on autonomous
software agents is rich in architectural diagrams and short on
formal definitions.  An agent is variously described as "a
system that perceives and acts", "a planner that calls tools",
or "an LLM in a loop", and each definition is stated at a level
of abstraction that admits neither a decision procedure nor a
complexity bound.  The consequence is that two agents with
radically different computational power are routinely compared
as if they belonged to a single class, and verification claims
about agentic systems are correspondingly difficult to falsify.

We are not the first to ask whether the Chomsky hierarchy is the
right scaffold for thinking about this problem.  In particular,
Koohestani, Li, Podkopaev, and Izadi pose the question
Are agents just automata? koohestani2025automata
and answer affirmatively, establishing a memory-based equivalence
between simple-reflex agents and finite automata, hierarchical
task-decomposition agents and pushdown automata, and
unrestricted-memory agents and Turing
machines turing1936computable.  We accept their answer.
Their question, however, is the preliminary one.  The question
that does practical work is operational: given that agents
are automata, how can the Chomsky type of one agent be used to
bound the Chomsky type of another?  This is the
producer-consumer problem of
Dijkstra dijkstra1968cooperating re-stated in the language
of formal grammars.  The present paper offers a methodology that
answers it in the affirmative, with software verification and
validation techniques inferred into the consumer side and the
idealised theoretical apparatus of automata built into the
producer side.

Why a narrow definition

We deliberately keep the definition of an agent narrow, taking
only what is forced upon us by the theory of automata Turing
introduced in 1936 turing1936computable and the language
hierarchy Chomsky introduced in
1956-59 chomsky1956three,chomsky1959certain.  An agent,
for us, is an effectful machine that, when started on an
admissible input, emits a finite string of operation symbols and
then halts (or fails to halt).  The collection of all such
strings, taken across the input domain, is the agent's
trace language.  Two agents with the same trace language
are, by stipulation, the same agent.  Whatever cannot be read off
from the trace language is, for the purposes of this paper, not
treated as an agent property.

The motivation for keeping the definition narrow is twofold.
First, it gives a direct line from the agent to the Chomsky
hierarchy and to the recognising automata.  An agent whose trace
language is regular (Type-3) is realisable by a finite-state
controller and admits decidable verification in linear
time rabin1959finite.  An agent whose trace language is
context-free (Type-2) is realisable by a pushdown controller and
admits  membership hopcroft2006automata.  An agent
whose trace language is recursively enumerable (Type-0) is, in
the worst case, undecidable to verify - a fact whose
modern statement we inherit from Robinson's resolution of
Hilbert's tenth
problem robinson1949definability,davis1961decision, which
established that recursively enumerable sets are exactly the
Diophantine sets and that the membership problem for them admits
no general algorithm.

Second, the narrow definition gives an empirical handle.  If the
agent is its trace language, then the trace language can be
recorded, stored, and compared; experiments produce strings, and
those strings can be classified by the smallest grammar class
that generates them.  This places the VV of agentic systems on
the same footing as the VV of compilers, parsers, and protocol
implementations aho1972theory,hoare1969axiomatic -
domains in which the formal-language framing has, for half a
century, been the foundation of decidable correctness.

The Goal-Driven producer and Data-Driven consumer

Our central architectural claim is the producer-consumer
restatement.  There is exactly one Goal-Driven producer
agent.  Its trace language is Type-0: recursively enumerable
and, in general, undecidable.  In practice it is realised as a
large language model wrapped in a tool-calling loop, and the
loop is permitted to enumerate any finite-length sequence of
tool invocations from the union of the sub-agents' alphabets.
The Goal-Driven producer has no a priori knowledge of what
the goal is; it knows only the specification of the input domain
and the tool catalogue.

There is exactly one Data-Driven consumer agent, the
verifier.  Its trace language is Type-3: regular and, by
Rabin and Scott's construction rabin1959finite, decidable
in linear time.  The verifier is concretely realised as the
deterministic finite automaton of an Aho-Corasick keyword
index aho1975corasick compiled from the competition's
empirical fixtures: the unit-test corpus, the submission-validity
tests, the column-name checks, and the integrity tests on the
train.csv/test.csv/samplesubmission.csv
triple.

The binding is the entirety of our architectural claim:
quote
A single Goal-Driven Type-0 producer, tamed by a single
Data-Driven Type-3 consumer, results in success.
quote
Type-2 and Type-1 sub-agents are not designed; they are
derived.  They appear as the canonical projections of the
recursively enumerated trace onto the parts of the orchestration
substrate that admit, respectively, a stack discipline (the
back-edges of the planner/developer/reviewer loop, the
recursive retryphaseonlowscore) and a
linear-bounded workspace (the deterministic Aho-Corasick scanner
and the LBA-style computedependencygraph that walks
the phase-to-phase dependency relation).  We make these
projections precise in Section sec:chomsky.

A subtlety: nothing in the framework prevents a Type-3 agent from
itself acting as a producer, in which case it backward-infers
constrained changes onto the tape (e.g., updates to the keyword
set  as new task fixtures arrive).  Such a Type-3 producer is
admissible provided the inference terminates in a
decidable verdict.  When it does not, the classical halting
problem of Turing turing1936computable re-asserts itself
on the Type-3 producer's loop and the architecture loses its
verification budget.  We discuss the engineering consequence in
Section sec:discussion: in the realised system, decidability
is enforced by the regular envelope of the verifier and, where
the data-driven side begins to drift, by a final human
verification and validation step that closes the loop.

The start language and its recursive enumeration

The system is initialised with a single start language, given concretely in
data/tracelanguageoriginal.csv.  The start language
contains twelve sub-agents and their seed action alphabets -
nothing else.  The Goal-Driven Type-0 producer, bound by the
Data-Driven Type-3 consumer, then recursively enumerates
 onto the Spaceship Titanic task instance.  The
result is the trace language, recorded in
data/tracelanguagekaggle.csv.   contains
thirteen sub-agents.  The thirteenth,
kaggletrainer, is the emergent agent: it does not
exist in  and is materialised by the act of
enumeration, after the verifier first refuses traces that omit
the training step.

We claim that this enumeration is the operational meaning of
"Type-0 recursive enumeration" in the agentic setting.
Turing's machines enumerate strings; Robinson's theorem connects
the enumerated sets to Diophantine arithmetic; our agent
enumerates tool-call strings, and the connection between the
enumerated strings and the empirical Kaggle competition is
mediated by the Aho-Corasick verifier.

Idealisation and physical bounding

It is worth being clear about the difference between the theory
and the machine that actually ran the experiment.  The
Goal-Driven agent's trace language is idealised Type-0.
The actual execution was bounded by the finite tape and finite
RAM of a single laptop and, for the heavier ablation runs, by
the High Performance Computing Center at Texas Tech University.
The realised machine is, formally, a (very large) linear-bounded
automaton - Type-1.  The idealisation is sound for our
purposes: every trace producible by the realised LBA is also
producible by the idealised Turing machine, and the verified
trace language is, by closure under intersection at the
verifier's Chomsky level (Theorem thm:closure), bounded
above by Type-3.  The physical computation is therefore
far more restricted than the theoretical envelope, and any
verification claim we discharge about the idealised system holds
a fortiori on the bounded machine.  This is the standard
idealisation of
Hartmanis and Stearns hartmanis1965computational, and we
adopt it on those terms.

Related Work

The literature relevant to this paper falls into five strands.
The first asks whether agents are automata, and answers
affirmatively in a memory-architecture sense
(Koohestani et al. koohestani2025automata).  The second is
the producer-consumer framing of cooperating sequential
processes (Dijkstra dijkstra1968cooperating), which is
the operational form of the question we actually wish to answer:
how can the type of one agent bound another?  The third is the
classical theory of automata and the Chomsky hierarchy (Turing,
Chomsky, Rabin and Scott, Hopcroft and Ullman, Aho).  The fourth
is software verification and validation (Hoare, Dijkstra, Pnueli,
Clarke and Emerson, Lamport).  The fifth is the boundary
between decidable and undecidable verification fixed by Robinson
and the MRDP theorem.  We address each in turn.

Are agents just automata?

Koohestani, Li, Podkopaev, and Izadi koohestani2025automata
recently posed and answered the title question, establishing a
memory-architecture equivalence: simple-reflex agents are finite
automata; hierarchical task-decomposition agents are pushdown
automata; agents employing read/write memory for reflection are
Turing machines.  Their paper introduces a right-sizing
principle: choose the minimal computational class sufficient
for the task.

Two observations are owed to that paper.  First, the equivalence
they establish is the architectural premise of the present work;
without it, the question of how Chomsky classes interact across
agents would have no canonical formulation.  Second, their paper
is preliminary in scope: it classifies agents but does not
prescribe a construction in which one agent's class
constrains another's class.  We supply that construction
here.  Where they ask Are agents just automata? and
answer yes, we ask can the type of one agent bound
another? and answer yes, with VV techniques inferred
into a Data-Driven verifier and the idealised theoretical
apparatus of automata built into a Goal-Driven producer.

A related strand, Formal-LLM li2024formalllm, encodes
plan-construction constraints as a context-free grammar and
guides an LLM-based planner with a pushdown automaton.  Our
construction is dual:  we do not constrain plan generation
through a higher-class grammar, we verify plan emission
through a lower-class one.  Aho-Corasick is the
"less-Turing-complete-than-the-LLM" device that earns us the
verification budget of Theorem thm:closure.

The producer-consumer framing

Dijkstra's cooperating sequential
processes dijkstra1968cooperating fix the producer-
consumer formulation:  one process produces a sequence of
artefacts; another consumes them; correct cooperation requires
that the consumer never reads an artefact the producer has not
yet produced, and the producer never overwrites an artefact the
consumer has not yet consumed.  The classical question is:
what synchronisation discipline is sufficient?  In our
setting the question becomes: what Chomsky-class discipline
on the consumer is sufficient to render the producer's
recursively enumerable output decidable in finite time?  The
answer, by Theorem thm:closure, is Type-3.  Lower would not
work (Type-3 is the minimal class that contains the keyword
matchers Aho-Corasick implements); higher would work but raise
the verification budget unnecessarily and risk the halting
problem returning through the consumer's loop.

Automata theory and the Chomsky hierarchy

Turing's 1936 paper turing1936computable introduces the
abstract machine that bears his name and proves the existence of
a universal one.  Every formal claim in this paper inherits, at
some depth, from that construction.  Chomsky's 1956 and 1959
papers chomsky1956three,chomsky1959certain fix the
hierarchy itself: regular, context-free, context-sensitive, and
unrestricted (Type-3 through Type-0).  Rabin and
Scott rabin1959finite fix the formal correspondence
between regular languages and finite automata, with the
nondeterminism that makes the proofs tractable.  Hopcroft,
Motwani, and Ullman hopcroft2006automata consolidate the
modern treatment with strict inclusions and standard parsing
complexities.  Hartmanis and
Stearns hartmanis1965computational introduce the time and
space complexity classes in which our Type-1/Type-0 distinction
ultimately lives, and supply the formal vocabulary for our
"idealised Type-0 versus realised LBA" discussion in
Section sec:discussion.

Aho's 1968 paper aho1968indexed introduces indexed
grammars as a strict extension of context-free grammars,
positioned strictly between Type-2 and Type-1.  In our framework
the back-edge structure of an iterative repair loop - e.g.,
the retryphaseonlowscore cycle in
Section sec:methodology - inhabits exactly that zone.  We
use Aho's indexed grammars as the bridge between the pushdown
sub-agent (Type-2) and the linear-bounded sub-agent (Type-1).

The Aho-Corasick paper aho1975corasick compiles a finite
keyword set into a deterministic finite automaton that recognises
the union of the keywords in linear time and constant space.  In
our framework Aho-Corasick is the canonical realisation
of the Data-Driven Type-3 consumer.

Hoare's axiomatic basis hoare1969axiomatic and Dijkstra's
guarded-command calculus dijkstra1975guarded supply the
machinery of Hoare triples  that we apply to
each unit-test step in the Aho-Corasick keyword set.  The
predicates  and  are the "before" and "after" states

Pnueli's 1977 paper pnueli1977temporal introduces the
linear temporal logic in which we phrase the safety and

expresses that every plan emitted by the producer is eventually

and Clarke, Grumberg, and Peled clarke1999modelchecking
consolidate the model-checking framework that, in principle,
decides the Type-3 component of our verifier.

Lamport lamport1978clocks,lamport1994tla supplies the

The undecidability boundary: Robinson and MRDP

The boundary between decidable and undecidable verification -
which, in our framework, is the boundary between Type-1 and
Type-0 agents - is fixed by Hilbert's tenth problem and its
resolution.  Robinson's 1949 paper robinson1949definability
and the Davis-Putnam-Robinson follow-up davis1961decision
established that the recursively enumerable subsets of
 are exactly the Diophantine subsets, and that
the membership problem for an arbitrary recursively enumerable
set admits no general algorithm.  The Goal-Driven Type-0 producer
in our architecture inherits this undecidability directly: in the
absence of the Type-3 verifier, no algorithm decides whether a
candidate trace lies in the producer's trace language.  The
verifier is the entire reason the system is decidable in
practice.

Complexity, learning, and symbolic AI

Cook cook1971complexity and Karp karp1972reducibility
fix the NP-completeness frame within which the model-search step
of the Goal-Driven producer (the Optuna tuning of LightGBM, the
cross-validated stacking ensemble) is, formally, an instance of
constrained combinatorial optimisation.
Yao yao1977probabilistic supplies the notion of expected
complexity used when reporting average trace lengths over the
input distribution.  Blum blum1967machine fixes the
machine-independent measure in which we report the verified
trace language's complexity.

The supervised-learning component of the Spaceship Titanic
solution is justified by the deep-learning manifesto of LeCun,
Bengio, and Hinton hinton2015deep; the causal discipline
that prevents the verifier from being fooled by spurious
correlations is justified by Pearl pearl2009causality.
McCarthy mccarthy1960lisp is the symbolic representation
of programs as data - the property that lets a Goal-Driven
agent emit a program (a Kaggle notebook) as the terminal
element of its trace.  Newell and Simon newell1976physical
state the physical-symbol-system hypothesis under which the
agent's tool calls are the elementary symbols.  We adopt their
hypothesis as a working assumption rather than as a contribution.

Synthesis

The strands above suffice to support every formal claim in the
sequel.  In particular, our novelty relative to
Koohestani et al. koohestani2025automata is the
producer-consumer construction: where they classify, we bound;
where they say which class an agent inhabits, we say which class
must consume an agent of class  in order for the system
as a whole to remain decidable.  The empirical instantiation in
Sections sec:expsetup-sec:results is reported as

Recursive enumeration and undecidability

A language  is recursively enumerable
(r.e., or semi-decidable) if there exists a Turing machine
 such that, started on the empty input,  prints exactly the
strings of  on its output tape (in some order, possibly with
repetitions).  Equivalently,  is r.e. iff there exists a Turing
machine  that halts and accepts on input  iff,
and may run forever on.

A language  is decidable (recursive) if both
and its complement  are r.e.; equivalently, if there
exists a Turing machine that halts on every input and accepts iff.  The strict inclusion of decidable r.e. languages
inside r.e. languages is a theorem of
Turing turing1936computable; the undecidability of the
membership problem for arbitrary r.e. languages is the
Halting Problem, also Turing's.

A celebrated and non-elementary characterisation of the r.e. class
is Robinson's:  the r.e. subsets of  are exactly the
Diophantine subsets robinson1949definability,davis1961decision.
The corollary - that there is no algorithm deciding membership
in an arbitrary r.e. set defined by Diophantine constraints -
is the form in which we use the result.  An idealised Type-0
agent therefore inherits, by the same line of argument, the
property that no algorithm decides whether a given trace is
in the agent's trace language.

Idealised versus physically bounded machines

Every physical computation is bounded: the tape is a finite array
of cells, the time is finite, the energy is finite.  The
idealisation we adopt is the standard one of
Hartmanis and Stearns hartmanis1965computational: we
analyse machines whose tape and time are unbounded in the formal
specification, while observing that any concrete execution
witnesses only a finite prefix of the idealised computation.

For a Turing machine, fix a tape bound  and a
time bound.  The bounded machine  is the

Discussion

We discuss five matters: the meaning of "idealised Type-0" on a
laptop, the role of the start language, the producer-consumer
restatement and the subtlety of a Type-3 producer, threats to
validity, and the relationship between the trace-language theory
and the prior literature on agent architectures.

Idealised Type-0 versus realised LBA

The Goal-Driven producer's trace language is, on paper,
recursively enumerable.  In practice, the producer runs on a
single laptop with a finite tape and a finite execution budget,
supplemented by the High Performance Computing Center at
Texas Tech University for the heavier ablation runs.  The
realised machine is therefore a (very large) linear-bounded
automaton - strictly Type-1.  We claim the Type-0 framing is
nonetheless the right one for the analysis, on the basis of
Lemma lem:bounded-soundness: every trace that the bounded
machine emits is also a trace of the idealised machine, and the
verification claims we discharge against the idealised machine
apply a fortiori to the bounded one.

The asymmetry matters in only one direction.  Were we to claim a
lower bound on the producer's expressive power - e.g.,
that it can recognise an arbitrary recursively enumerable
language - the laptop bound would refute the claim.  We make
no such claim.  The Type-0 framing is used upper, not
lower:  it is the smallest class within which the producer's
enumeration behaviour fits, and it is the class within which the
undecidability shadow of
Robinson's MRDP theorem robinson1949definability,davis1961decision
applies.  The Type-3 consumer brings the verification problem
back inside the decidable, and the laptop bound brings the
actual execution back inside the finite; both are sound
restrictions.

In Hartmanis-Stearns'
language hartmanis1965computational, the realised machine
inhabits a complexity class strictly inside Type-1.  In
Turing's turing1936computable language, it is a finite
restriction of a universal machine.  Both views are correct;
neither is more correct than the other.

Why the start language is necessary

It would be tempting to dispense with the start language and ask
the Goal-Driven producer to invent its own operation alphabet.
We attempted this in pilot runs and found, predictably, that the
producer's enumeration drifts into pathological corners of
 that the consumer was not designed to cover.  The
start language  serves two formal roles:
enumerate
It fixes the seed alphabet  over which the
consumer's keyword set  is defined.  Without, the
Aho-Corasick DFA cannot be constructed.
It provides a finite syntactic skeleton from which the
Type-0 enumeration can extend in a structured way.  The producer
treats  as a base case for its recursion, not as a fixed
ceiling.
enumerate
The start language is therefore the inductive hypothesis of the
recursive enumeration; without it, the recursion has no base.
The empirical CSV tracelanguageoriginal.csv is the
artefact form of the inductive hypothesis.  The realised CSV
tracelanguagekaggle.csv is the conclusion of one
inductive step.  More inductive steps (one per task instance)
would produce more emergent columns; the architecture
generalises straightforwardly to the full MLE-bench
suite.

Producer-consumer, and the Type-3 producer subtlety

The Koohestani-Li-Podkopaev-Izadi
question koohestani2025automata asks what class is
this agent? and stops there.  The producer-consumer
question of Dijkstra dijkstra1968cooperating asks
how do classes interact? - which is the
question this paper answers.  In the canonical setting, the
producer runs the harder enumeration and the consumer runs the
cheaper check.  In our binding, the producer is Type-0 and the
consumer is Type-3, and the verification budget falls to
per candidate trace by Theorem thm:closure.

A natural question follows.  May a Type-3 agent itself act
as a producer?  The answer is yes, with one essential condition.
Concretely, in our system the verifier may discover a new
fixture (e.g., a new column-name keyword as the train CSV schema
is augmented mid-run) and push that keyword into the
keyword set.  This is a backward inference from the data
side onto the tape: the regular consumer becomes, locally, a
producer that constrains the principal producer's future
emissions.

The condition is decidability.  The Type-3 producer's loop must
terminate in a decidable verdict, otherwise the classical
halting problem of Turing turing1936computable
re-asserts itself on the consumer side and the architecture
loses its verification budget.  Concretely:  if the
keyword-update rule is itself Type-0 (e.g., "add a keyword if
the LLM judges it relevant"), the rule's trace language is
undecidable, and there exist runs in which the keyword set never
stabilises, the verifier never accepts, and the system never
terminates.  This is the producer side of the halting problem;
the architecture loses no theory, but it loses its operational
budget.

The escape is to make the keyword-update rule itself Type-3
- i.e., a regular grammar over a finite catalogue of
admissible keyword extensions - or, where this is infeasible,
to insert a final human verification and validation step
that closes the loop.  In the realised system, both escapes are
used.  The test* extensions are admitted by a regular
grammar read off the train CSV schema; the leaderboard
submission itself is reviewed by the first author before upload.
The system is therefore a Type-3-bounded Type-0 producer in the
inner loop, and a human-in-the-loop closure on the outer loop.
Both are sound; neither, alone, would be.

This is the operational meaning of "yes, with software
verification and validation techniques inferred into the
Data-Driven agent."  The VV techniques are precisely the
machinery of Hoare hoare1969axiomatic,
Dijkstra dijkstra1975guarded,
Pnueli pnueli1977temporal, and
Clarke-Emerson clarke1981ctl,clarke1999modelchecking
applied at the regular level of the Aho-Corasick consumer.
They are not a metaphor; they are the keyword set, the DFA, and the LTL formula.

Threats to validity

Construct validity.
The trace-language identity criterion (Section sec:trace)
is a strong claim: an agent is nothing but its trace
language.  The criterion is well-defined - two agents are
identified iff  - but it
does discard information about, e.g., the LLM's chain-of-thought
or the planner's intermediate JSON.  We accept the discard
because the discarded objects are not directly verifiable; only
the trace is, and only the trace is what we admit as evidence.

Internal validity.
The Goal-Driven producer's LLM core is non-deterministic.  Two
re-runs of Algorithm alg:enum on the same input produce
slightly different traces.  The class signature
(Table tab:class-signature) is robust across re-runs in
our pilot experiments, but the leaderboard placement varies by
10-20 ranks across re-runs.  We report a single placement
() and acknowledge the residual variance.  The
reproducibility kit at

https://www.kaggle.com/sweeden-ttu/competitions/spaceship-titanic

allows external re-runs at fixed random seeds.

External validity.
The architecture has been instantiated on one task
(Spaceship Titanic).  We expect the trace-language
account to generalise to other Kaggle-style tabular tasks
(House Prices, Titanic,
Digit Recognizer), because the start language and
consumer are task-agnostic.  We do not claim generalisation
outside the tabular regime; agents that operate on images, text,
or open-ended code may require an expanded start language and a
correspondingly expanded keyword set.

Coverage of the producer-consumer escape.
The discussion in Section sec:type3-producer relies on
either (a) a regular keyword-update rule, or (b) a human in the
loop.  In our experiments both held; we do not claim a general
solution when neither holds.  The recursion-without-decidability
case is exactly where the halting problem returns and the
architecture provides no warranty.

Implications for the field

Two implications follow.  First, the formal-language framing of
agents need not be metaphorical.  When done at the level of
narrowness of this paper, it produces concrete bounds on
verification cost (Theorem thm:closure,
Corollary cor:budget) and operational architectures (one
Type-0 producer, one Type-3 consumer) that are competitive with
the contemporary state of the art.

Second, the multiplication of agent "roles" in contemporary
literature - planner, executor, critic, verifier, summariser
- is, at least in our case, an emergent phenomenon, not an
architectural choice.  The architect designs two agents; the
binding produces five more.  This is consistent with
McCarthy's mccarthy1960lisp programs-as-data and
Newell-Simon's newell1976physical symbol-system
hypothesis: roles are projections, not primitives.  We
conjecture, but do not here prove, that other contemporary agent
architectures admit a similar derivation: a small number of
principal agents binds a verifier, and the named roles are
projections of the verified trace.

What we did not do

We did not measure the LLM's chain-of-thought.  We did not
compare backbone families.  We did not run a full ablation over
the keyword set.  We did not formally prove that the realised
trace is the unique fixed point of the binding.  Each is a
defensible piece of follow-up work; none is required for the
present claim, which is that a narrow theory of trace
languages, derived from Turing's automata theory and bound by an
Aho-Corasick consumer, suffices to produce a top-tertile
leaderboard result on a contemporary ML-engineering benchmark.

Conclusion

Koohestani et al. koohestani2025automata asked whether
agents are just automata; we accept their answer and move on to
the operational question: can the Chomsky type of one agent be
used to bound the type of another?  We have given a self-contained
formal theory that answers yes, derived from
Turing's turing1936computable 1936 theory of automata and
Chomsky's 1956-59 hierarchy chomsky1956three,
chomsky1959certain, in which an agent is identified with, and
only with, its trace language.  Within this theory:

Future work

Four directions are immediate.  First, the architecture should
be evaluated on the remaining tasks of MLE-bench to
test the external-validity claim of
Section sec:discussion.  Second, the start language should
be examined as a design object in its own right: which start
languages produce stable bindings, and which fail to converge?
Third, the trace-language identity criterion of
Definition def:traceeq invites a formal study of agent
equivalence: when are two agents with different LLM backbones
trace-equivalent on a given task?  Fourth, suggested by the
Section sec:type3-producer discussion, is to characterise
the class of regular keyword-update rules that preserve
decidability under a Type-3 producer; we conjecture this class
is exactly the regular extensions of the start-language alphabet.

Closing remark

Turing wrote in 1936 that "the justification [for the
definitions] lies in the fact that the human memory is
necessarily limited" turing1936computable.  Our laptop's
memory is necessarily limited.  Our agent's tape is necessarily
limited.  Within those limits, the theory developed in this
paper is exact; outside them, it is the natural idealisation.

A final note.  This paper began as a question in an email about
arithmetic grammars and grew under conditions in which other
deliverables were not available to the first author - a
sequence we record honestly in the Acknowledgments.  Papers are
sometimes written in a straight line and sometimes in a crooked
one, and the architecture proposed here was, in a real sense,
shaped by both: the formal narrowness of the theory, and the
narrow window in which the theory had to be made to do real
work.  We close by recording the conviction that an agent is its
trace language - nothing more, nothing less - and that
narrowness, paired with a producer-consumer discipline that
bounds Type-0 by Type-3, is enough.

Recognising Julia B. Robinson

A note is owed on the most consequential non-laureate citation
of this paper.  Julia Bowman Robinson (1919-1985) is the
mathematician whose work, with Martin Davis, Hilary Putnam, and
Yuri Matiyasevich, established that the recursively enumerable
subsets of  are exactly the Diophantine subsets
- the modern characterisation of the recursively enumerable
class and the resolution of Hilbert's tenth
problem robinson1949definability,davis1961decision.  The
central undecidability claim that motivates our two-tier
Type-0/Type-3 architecture is, in its modern form, her
result.

Robinson did not receive the ACM A.M. Turing Award.  We are
under no illusion as to why.  The Award was first conferred in
1966 and through 2025 has been given overwhelmingly to men -
a pattern that the field's own histories now acknowledge as a
reflection of decades of structural bias in who was admitted to
graduate study, hired into faculty positions, nominated for
prizes, and otherwise allowed to participate in the
institutional life of computer science and mathematics.
Several of Robinson's contemporaries did receive the Award; she
did not.  Her work, however, is foundational to this paper, and
we cite it as the foundational contribution it is.  Robinson is
listed in the bibliography alongside Turing, Chomsky, and Aho
not as an exception but because she belongs there on the
mathematics.

Robinson was the first woman elected to the National Academy of
Sciences in mathematics (1976), the first woman President of the
American Mathematical Society (1983), and a MacArthur Fellow
(1983).  We name her contributions in the form they took: the
Robinson hypothesis, the
Davis-Putnam-Robinson theorem, the resolution of
Hilbert's tenth.  Where the field's awards have been silent, the
mathematics is not.
